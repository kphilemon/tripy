\n Japan on brink of recession\n

\n
\n
\n \n \n
\n
    \n
  • \n Economy\n \n \n \n
    • \n
  • \n

    \n Tuesday, 18 Feb 2020\n

\n\n\n\nEconomy shrinks: A file picture showing shipping containers at a port in Tokyo. Japan\xe2\x80\x99s GDP shrank an annualised 6.3% in the October-December period, much faster than a median market forecast for a 3.7% drop and the first decline in five quarters. \xe2\x80\x94 Reuters

\n
\n
\n \n
\n \n \n TOKYO: Japan\xe2\x80\x99s economy shrank at the fastest pace in almost six years in the December quarter as a sales tax hike hit consumer and business spending, raising the risk of a recession as China\xe2\x80\x99s coronavirus outbreak chills global activity.

Analysts say the widening fallout from the epidemic, which is damaging output and tourism, could have a significant impact on Japan if it\xe2\x80\x99s not contained in coming months.

\xe2\x80\x9cThere\xe2\x80\x99s a pretty good chance the economy will suffer another contraction in January-March.

\xe2\x80\x9cThe virus will mainly hit inbound tourism and exports, but could also weigh on domestic consumption quite a lot,\xe2\x80\x9d said Taro Saito, executive research fellow at NLI Research Institute.

\xe2\x80\x9cIf this epidemic is not contained by the time of the Tokyo Olympic Games, the damage to the economy will be huge,\xe2\x80\x9d he said.

Japan\xe2\x80\x99s gross domestic product (GDP) shrank an annualised 6.3% in the October-December period, government data showed yesterday, much faster than a median market forecast for a 3.7% drop and the first decline in five quarters.

It was the biggest fall since the second quarter of 2014, when consumption took a hit from a sales tax hike in April of that year.

The weak data also comes amid signs of struggle in the wider region with the coronavirus, leaving Japan vulnerable to a recession - defined as two consecutive quarters of contraction.

Singapore cut its economic growth projections for 2020, Thailand posted its slowest expansion in five years and China\xe2\x80\x99s home prices rose at their weakest pace in almost two years.

Japanese stocks slipped on the recession prospects with the benchmark Nikkei average 0.7% down and the broader Topix 0.9% off by the close.

The sales tax hike in October last year - as well as unusually warm weather that hurt sales of winter items - weighed on private consumption, which sank a bigger-than-expected 2.9%, marking the first drop in five quarters.

Capital expenditure fell 3.7% in the fourth quarter, much faster than a median forecast for a 1.6% drop and the first decline in three quarters, the data showed.

Combined, domestic demand knocked 2.1 percentage points off GDP growth, more than offsetting a 0.5 point contribution from external demand.

Japanese policymakers had warned that the economy will suffer a contraction in October-December as the sales tax hike, typhoons and the Sino-U.S. trade war hurt consumption and factory output.

Still, the weakness in capital expenditure - considered among the few bright spots in the economy \xe2\x80\x93 casts doubt on the Bank of Japan\xe2\x80\x99s view that growth will continue to expand moderately as robust domestic demand makes up for faltering exports.

Last month, the BOJ kept monetary policy steady.

BOJ governor Haruhiko Kuroda said the central bank would consider additional easing if the coronavirus outbreak significantly threatened Japan\xe2\x80\x99s economy and price trends, the Sankei newspaper reported on Monday.

In his interview with the daily, Kuroda said the outbreak was the \xe2\x80\x9cbiggest uncertainty\xe2\x80\x9d for the domestic economy.

Worries about the spread of the coronavirus and its hit to the global economy kept Japanese manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 mood gloomy in February, a Reuters poll found.

The outbreak has forced factories in China to shut down and led to a sharp drop in Chinese tourists.

Still, many analysts doubt whether the government and the central bank have effective means to fight another recession given their dwindling policy ammunition.

\xe2\x80\x9cThe government has already taken steps to respond to the sales tax hike and post-Olympics slowdown, so you cannot expect further steps on the fiscal front,\xe2\x80\x9d said Takeshi Minami, chief economist at Norinchukin Research Institute.

\xe2\x80\x9cThere\xe2\x80\x99s not a lot more the BOJ can do either ... Additional easing may do more harm than good to the economy.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x94 Reuters
