\n\t\tHong Kong economy shrinks for first time in a decade\t

\n\t\n\t
\n\t\t
\n\t\t\tby AFP\t\t\t18:00, 3 February 202023:57, 31 March 2020\t\t
\n\t\t
\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t
\n\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t
\n\n\t\t\t\t\t\n
\n\t
\n\t\t\n

Hong Kong confirmed Monday it plunged into recession in 2019, suffering its first annual contraction in a decade as the city buckled under the twin pressures of the US-China\xc2\xa0trade war and months of furious pro-democracy protests.

\n\n\n\n

The data was released as the semi-autonomous city begins the lunar new year facing another economic crisis \xe2\x80\x94 the outbreak of a deadly new coronavirus strain which began on the Chinese mainland and is compounding Hong Kong\xe2\x80\x99s economic woes.

\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n

Last year was the worst for Hong Kong\xe2\x80\x99s growth since 2009, when the financial hub was hit hard by the global financial crash.

\n\n\n\n

The crisis was initially sparked by the trade war between Beijing and Washington, hitting the export-heavy economy.

\n\n\n\n

But the hard times were made worse by months of seething and often violent protests seeking greater democracy and police accountability.

\n\n\n\n

Official figures released on Monday showed Hong Kong\xe2\x80\x99s gross domestic product shrank 1.2 percent on-year for 2019.

\n\n\n\n
\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n

The fourth quarter \xe2\x80\x94 which witnessed the worst violence between police and protesters \xe2\x80\x94 clocked a 2.9 percent contraction, the third straight quarter of declines.

\n\n\n\n

\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Heavy toll\xe2\x80\x99

\n\n\n\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe local social incidents with violence during the quarter took a further heavy toll on economic sentiment as well as consumption and tourism related activities,\xe2\x80\x9d the government said in a statement.

\n\n\n\n

\xe2\x80\x9cTotal\xc2\xa0exports of goods\xc2\xa0continued to decline amid the difficult external environment, though at a somewhat narrower rate,\xe2\x80\x9d it added.

\n\n\n\n

Clashes between protesters hurling bricks and petrol bombs and police wielding tear gas and rubber bullets became a weekly occurrence for nearly six months last year.

\n\n\n\n

The protests have died down in frequency and ferocity in the last two months, but the underlying cause has not gone away.

\n\n\n\n

Hong Kongers are rattled by the feeling that an increasingly assertive Beijing is clamping down on their city\xe2\x80\x99s freedoms.

\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n

The protests were first sparked by a proposal to allow extraditions to the mainland and its opaque, Communist Party-controlled legal system.

\n\n\n\n

But they soon morphed into a popular revolt against Beijing\xe2\x80\x99s rule.

\n\n\n\n

The extradition bill was eventually killed off after months of unrest, but the movement has achieved few of its other demands, which include an inquiry into the police, an amnesty for those arrested and fully free elections.

\n\n\n\n

Economists see little reason for cheer in the months ahead.

\n\n\n\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe coronavirus outbreak will probably keep the city in recession for a while longer,\xe2\x80\x9d Capital Economics wrote in a note to clients after the GDP figures were released.

\n\n\n\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe virus outbreak will certainly dampen any green shoots,\xe2\x80\x9d added Raymond Yeung at ANZ. \xe2\x80\x9cWe will see a bigger negative in the first quarter (of 2020).\xe2\x80\x9d
